Wave Functions

A grid of particles, each one lifted by a wave and colored by its height. The catch is that the waves aren't decoration — every phase is a real physical model, and the piece cycles through four of them, 30 seconds each. Click the equation in the corner to pause and read the math behind whatever is on screen; click the surface itself to drop a ripple.

Four ways to move a grid

The wave equation — the classic ∂²u/∂t² = c²∇²u, solved directly on the grid with a finite-difference step. Drops radiate as expanding rings, reflect off the edges, and interfere. d'Alembert wrote it down in 1747 for a vibrating string; a century later Maxwell found the same equation hiding inside electromagnetism, which is how we learned light is a wave.

Quantum |ψ|² — superposed energy states of a particle trapped in a box, each spinning at its own frequency e−iEₙt/ħ. The height is the probability of finding the particle there. Schrödinger wrote the equation in 1926; Max Born realized the same year that |ψ|² is a probability. It's the math behind atomic orbitals, transistors, lasers, and quantum tunneling.

Chladni modes — the standing-wave shapes a vibrating plate is allowed to take. Along the nodal lines the surface never moves; sprinkle sand on a real plate and it gathers there, drawing the pattern. Ernst Chladni demonstrated this in 1787, and the problem of explaining it helped launch the theory of elasticity. Engineers still use it to tune instruments and to hunt down destructive resonances.

Gerstner waves — a model of ocean swell where each water particle travels in a circle rather than just bobbing, which is what gives the sea its sharp crests and flat troughs. Gerstner found it in 1802; it's one of the few exact wave solutions of fluid flow, and it's the ancestor of the oceans rendered in films and games.

How it's built

A single HTML file: 40,000 particles in a 200×200 lattice, the height field recomputed every frame on the CPU, rendered with three.js and a little bloom. The wave-equation field integrates continuously in the background so ripples carry across the blends between phases. The equations in the panel render with KaTeX.
